Visual Explorations

Interactive geometry of cohesive \(\infty\)-topoi, quantum modalities, and the obstruction theorem.

What this shows (plain English)

Each glowing dot is a possible state of a classical system. Drag them — the centre comonad collapses all states to a single classical point.

Mathematical description

The line represents the Gelfand spectrum of a commutative \(C^*\)-algebra. Under \(Q^\diamond\), the representable sheaf \(\mathbf{y}_{M_2}\) becomes empty; only commutative algebras survive. This interactive model shows that collapse: all points coalesce into the unique trivial character.

What this shows (plain English)

Adjust the spread with the slider — when spread is zero, the classical core is a single point, and quantum degrees of freedom vanish.

Mathematical description

The slider controls the dimension of the centre. For \(s=0\), the algebra is simple and non‑commutative; the hom‑set \(\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{C}, M_n)\) is empty, so the state space collapses to a point. For \(s>0\), multiple commutative summands exist, giving a larger spectrum.

What this shows (plain English)

Pinch‑zoom and drag the diagram. Click the diamond to see the obstruction flatten when the classical core is cartesian.

Mathematical description

The square encodes the naturality condition for a cloning map \(c:\mathbf{y}_{(-)}\to\mathbf{y}_{(-)}\otimes\mathbf{y}_{(-)}\). When the classical core is cartesian, the Seely isomorphism trivialises and the two sides of the square coincide.

What this shows (plain English)

A free‑sketch canvas: draw your own diagram and explore how the obstruction changes. Use mouse or touch to draw. Clear with double‑click.

Mathematical description

The same categorical square, but open for experimentation. The mathematical content is identical to the left panel.

What this shows (plain English)

Rotate, zoom, and pan the 3D scene. The dark spheres are geometric manifolds; the blue columns are algebraic Hilbert spaces. Click the diamond to see the quantum comonad project the columns to their commutative core.

Mathematical description

\(\mathbf{E} = \mathrm{Sh}(\mathbb{S}, J)\) with \(\mathbb{S} = \mathbf{Mfd} \times \mathrm{Hilb}_{\mathrm{CP}}^{\mathrm{op}}\). The spheres represent objects in \(\mathbf{Mfd}\); the columns represent \(\mathrm{Hilb}_{\mathrm{CP}}^{\mathrm{op}}\). \(Q^\diamond\) acts as \(i_! i^*\) with \(i: \mathbf{cCAlg}_{\mathrm{CP}}^{\mathrm{op}} \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Hilb}_{\mathrm{CP}}^{\mathrm{op}}\), collapsing each column to the classical subcategory.

What this shows (plain English)

A torus knot representing the mixed distributive law — twist it from every angle.

Mathematical description

The torus knot parametrises the entwining map \(\psi(g\otimes\delta_x) = \delta_{gxg^{-1}}\otimes g\) for \(G = S_3\). The knot’s crossings encode the four axioms of a mixed distributive law.

What this shows (plain English)

A tesseract (4D hypercube) rotating. The coloured edges show how the distributive law \(\lambda\) maps one monad‑comonad pair to the other.

Mathematical description

The 4D rotation illustrates the coherence cube of the mixed distributive law between \(T\) (group algebra monad) and \(S\) (dual coalgebra comonad). Each edge is a component of \(\lambda\).

What this shows (plain English)

A second tesseract showing the inverse distributive law. The winding edges show that the inverse exists because the entwining map is invertible.

Mathematical description

The inverse \(\lambda^{-1} = \psi^{-1}\otimes\mathrm{id}\) is constructed from the inverse entwining \(\psi^{-1}(\delta_y\otimes g)=g\otimes\delta_{g^{-1}yg}\). This tesseract visualises the symmetry.
